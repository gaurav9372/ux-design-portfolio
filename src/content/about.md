# About Page — Content Architecture
Edit this file to update the About page. Save and refresh to see changes.
Based on research from CareerFoundry, Designlab, Dribbble, UXPin, NN/g, Sarah Doody, and 10+ design portfolio guides.

---

## SECTION 1 — Hero / Opening Statement

Purpose: Bold first impression. The reader should know exactly who you are and what you do within 5 seconds.

Structure:
- Eyebrow label: "About me"
- Photo: A professional but approachable headshot (not overly corporate)
- Compass statement (1 line): A specific one-liner that communicates your niche and value. Not "I am a UX Designer" but something that says what kind of designer you are and who you design for.
- Example: "I design digital products that make complex things feel simple — from checkout flows to enterprise dashboards."
- Sub-line (1 sentence): Your location and current status. Example: "Based in Varanasi, India. Currently open to freelance and full-time opportunities."

Notes:
- Keep the compass statement consistent across LinkedIn, resume, and portfolio
- The photo builds trust and memorability — do not skip it
- This section should feel confident, not arrogant

---

## SECTION 2 — Short Bio / My Story

Purpose: Give the reader a reason to care. How did you get here? What drives you?

Structure:
- 2-3 short paragraphs (300 words max total)
- Paragraph 1: Your origin — how you got into design. What pulled you in. Keep it personal but brief.
- Paragraph 2: What you do now — your current focus, the kind of work you enjoy most, and the industries or project types you gravitate toward (e-commerce, SaaS, mobile apps, etc.)
- Paragraph 3: What makes you different — your unique perspective. This is where being from Varanasi, your specific process, or your cross-platform experience (web + mobile) becomes a differentiator.

Tone:
- First person, conversational, like talking to a colleague over coffee
- Active voice, short sentences
- No buzzwords ("passionate about creating delightful experiences" — everyone says this)
- Be specific — every sentence should say something only YOU could say

What to avoid:
- Wall of text — if it takes more than 45 seconds to read, it is too long
- Generic statements that apply to every designer
- Copying your resume — the bio tells a story, the resume lists facts

## SECTION 3 — Design Philosophy / Values

Purpose: Show maturity and self-awareness. This is what separates mid-level designers from juniors. Hiring managers look for evidence that you think about design, not just do it.

Structure:
- 3-4 core principles that guide your work
- Each one: a short bold title (3-5 words) + 1-2 sentences explaining what it means to you
- These should be specific to YOU, not generic truths everyone agrees with

Examples of BAD values (too generic):
- "I believe in user-centered design"
- "I focus on clean, minimal interfaces"
- "Good design is invisible"

Examples of GOOD values (specific, opinionated):
- "Research before rectangles" — I do not open Figma until I understand who I am designing for and why.
- "Constraints are direction" — Tight deadlines and technical limits are not problems. They tell me where to focus.
- "Ship and learn" — A good design shipped today beats a perfect design shipped never. I design for iteration, not perfection.
- "Developers are design partners" — My best work happens when I sit with the people who build what I design.

Format: Cards or a clean grid. Each value visually distinct with a title and body.

## SECTION 4 — Skills and Expertise

Purpose: Quick reference for what you can do. This section is scanned in 5 seconds, not read.

Structure:
- Lead with capabilities, not tools
- Group into 2-3 categories:
  - Design: User Research, Interaction Design, Prototyping, Visual Design, Design Systems, Information Architecture
  - Research: User Interviews, Usability Testing, Surveys, Competitive Analysis, Persona Development, Journey Mapping
  - Tools: Figma, FigJam, Maze, Miro, Hotjar, Notion (or whatever you actually use)

Format: Tags/pills (like the blog tags) or a clean 3-column grid. NOT a paragraph. NOT a bulleted list that goes on forever.

Notes:
- Only list skills you are genuinely confident in — do not pad the list
- Tools change, skills do not. Lead with skills.
- At mid-level, showing range (web + mobile, research + visual design) signals versatility

---

## SECTION 5 — Experience Highlights

Purpose: Social proof through work history. NOT a copy of your resume — this is the highlight reel.

Structure:
- 2-4 notable roles, companies, or clients
- For each: Company/Client name, your role, a 1-line description of what you did and the impact
- Include a quantified result where possible (e.g., "Redesigned checkout flow — +35% completion rate")
- If you have freelance experience, group it under a single entry like "Freelance UX Designer — 10+ clients across e-commerce, SaaS, and healthcare"

Format: A clean timeline or stacked cards. Each entry should be scannable in 3 seconds.

Alternative if you do not want a timeline:
- A "By the numbers" strip: Years of experience, projects completed, industries worked in, happy clients — similar to the stats section on the homepage but about page specific

Notes:
- Prioritize work that reflects where you WANT to go, not just where you have been
- If you did branding work but want product design roles, deprioritize the branding
- Company logos add credibility if you have recognizable ones

---

## SECTION 6 — Personal / Human Side

Purpose: Show you are a real person. Hiring managers at mid-level care about culture fit.

Structure:
- 1-2 sentences about life outside design
- Hobbies, interests, or a fun fact — but keep it brief and genuine
- A subtle connection to Varanasi or your personal context can be memorable
- Example: "When I am not designing, you will find me exploring Varanasi's old city lanes, binge-watching sci-fi shows, or arguing about which chai stall makes the best cup."

What to avoid:
- Long personal essays — this is a portfolio, not a memoir
- Listing every hobby — pick 2-3 that are genuinely interesting
- Anything that overshadows your professional identity

---

## SECTION 7 — Testimonials / Social Proof (optional but powerful)

Purpose: Let other people vouch for you. This is one of the strongest trust signals and most mid-level designers skip it.

Structure:
- 2-3 short quotes from colleagues, managers, or clients
- Each attributed with name and role
- Keep quotes under 2 sentences each — short and specific beats long and vague

Notes:
- Even quotes from peers or developers you worked with count
- If you do not have formal testimonials, skip this section rather than making them up
- Can reuse testimonials from the homepage if they exist

---

## SECTION 8 — Call to Action / Let's Connect

Purpose: Give the reader a clear next step. If someone reads your whole About page and cannot contact you, that is a wasted opportunity.

Structure:
- A clear heading: "Let's work together" or "Get in touch" or "Say hello"
- Email link (primary CTA)
- Links to LinkedIn, Behance/Dribbble, Twitter
- Optional: Resume/CV download link
- Keep it simple — one primary action, supporting links secondary

## PAGE-LEVEL NOTES

Layout and Visual Design:
- The About page itself should demonstrate your taste and skill — treat it as a design artifact
- Use plenty of whitespace — this is a reading-heavy page so breathing room matters
- Keep the whole page readable in under 2 minutes
- Mobile responsive is non-negotiable — hiring managers browse on phones

Recommended Visual Breaks:
- Photo in the hero
- Cards or grid for values and skills
- A different background shade for one section (e.g., testimonials) to break the monotony
- A decorative element or two (consistent with the rest of the site)

Content Priority (most important first):
1. Compass statement (who you are in one line)
2. Bio (your story in 2-3 paragraphs)
3. Design philosophy (what you believe)
4. Skills (what you can do)
5. Experience (where you have done it)
6. Personal (who you are outside work)
7. Testimonials (what others say)
8. CTA (how to reach you)

What NOT to Do:
- Do not use third person ("Shreyansh is a designer who..." — feels corporate and distant)
- Do not copy your resume onto the page
- Do not use excessive animations — if every element bounces in, it undermines your UX credibility
- Do not be vague — specificity is what makes an About page memorable
- Do not skip the photo
- Do not make the page longer than it needs to be — respect the reader's time
