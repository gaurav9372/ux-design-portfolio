## overview-summary
Legacy Square is a personal legacy-planning product built around one core job: helping users make a legally meaningful will without feeling overwhelmed by the process. Around that sits a broader digital vault for assets, liabilities, accounts, cards, contacts, family members, activities, and educational resources. The product had to balance seriousness and trust with enough warmth that users would actually complete the setup instead of delaying it.

## overview-contribution
I worked as the sole UX/UI designer on the product direction, information architecture, core flows, dashboard structure, vault modules, will-editor workflow, and visual system. My work covered discovery, user-flow design, navigation structure, wireframes, high-fidelity screens, reusable patterns, and handoff documentation for the development team.

---

## requirements-intro
The product needed to do more than look polished. The initial requirements were tightly tied to trust, clarity, and completion:

## requirement-1
Make will creation feel guided and manageable. Users needed a clear step-by-step flow instead of a legal process that felt dense or intimidating.

## requirement-2
Bring vault data and will data into one connected system. Assets, liabilities, contacts, and family members had to feed the will flow instead of living as isolated modules.

## requirement-3
Build trust quickly through structure, progress visibility, and calm visual design so users would feel safe storing sensitive information.

---

## problem-1
The product was trying to solve a heavy life event, but the experience did not provide enough guidance for first-time users.

## problem-2
Important modules existed, but their relationship to the main goal of creating a will was not obvious enough.

## problem-3
The dashboard surfaced information, but it needed stronger prioritization around what the user should do next.

## problem-4
Estate planning brings emotional hesitation. The interface had to reduce cognitive and emotional friction, not add to it.

## problem-5
Users were being asked for sensitive financial and family data, so trust signals and progress feedback had to be designed deliberately.

## problem-6
The Digital Vault covered many item types, but the patterns across assets, liabilities, accounts, and cards needed to feel consistent.

## problem-7
Contacts and family members were deeply connected to beneficiaries, executors, guardians, and witnesses, yet those relationships could easily become confusing.

## problem-8
The will flow had multiple dependencies and legal steps, so navigation needed to show both progress and what remained.

## problem-9
The product had educational resources and FAQs, but they needed to sit closer to the user's moment of need instead of feeling like a separate content area.

## problem-10
The subject matter was serious, but the UI still needed to feel approachable enough that users would continue instead of postponing the task.

---

## challenge-1
Designing for a high-friction emotional context. People often avoid will creation entirely, so the product had to feel supportive from the first screen.

## challenge-2
Bringing together many modules without making the navigation feel enterprise-heavy or confusing.

## challenge-3
Creating a system where structured data from the vault could naturally support the will-editor steps.

## challenge-4
Finding the right visual tone: serious enough for trust, but not cold, bureaucratic, or legalistic.

## challenge-5
Designing tables, trees, dashboards, and step-based flows under one visual system without the product feeling fragmented.

## challenge-6
Explaining complex legal and procedural concepts in plain language without oversimplifying what users needed to do.

## work-process
I treated the project as a product-architecture exercise first and a screen-design exercise second. The first phase focused on understanding the system: what data users needed to enter, how that data connected across modules, and which decisions were blocking will completion. Once that structure was clear, I mapped the main flows into a dashboard, a modular vault, and a guided editor so the product could support both setup work and ongoing maintenance.

## work-research
The discovery phase centered on how people approach legacy planning in real life: slowly, inconsistently, and often only after a trigger event. I reviewed estate-planning tools, secure-document products, fintech dashboards, and family-record tools to understand how trust, progress, and complexity were handled. The strongest pattern was clear guidance with visible progress, backed by modular data entry that could be completed in smaller pieces over time.

## work-ideation
I explored different navigation models, including a will-first flow, a vault-first flow, and a balanced model where the dashboard framed both. The balanced model worked best because it let users understand the broader system while still keeping "Make my will" as the dominant action. I also tested whether the stepper should be minimal or explicit. The explicit version won because users felt more confident when they could see every stage, its completion state, and the estimated time.

## work-solutions
The final solution combines a warm dashboard with a structured left-nav architecture. The dashboard prioritizes will completion, vault status, and high-level financial insight. The Digital Vault uses consistent table and creation patterns across assets, liabilities, accounts, and cards, reducing relearning. Contacts and family members are designed as reusable entities that can later be assigned will roles. The Will Editor uses a vertical stepper with progress percentages, sub-steps, and contextual guidance so the process feels sequential instead of abstract.

## research-intro
Research focused on two questions: what makes people hesitate to start legacy planning, and what makes them trust a product enough to keep going once they start.

## research-method-1
Competitive review of estate-planning, fintech, and secure-record products

## research-method-2
Flow audit of the existing information architecture and module relationships

## research-method-3
Task mapping for will creation, vault setup, and family/beneficiary linking

## research-method-4
Usability reviews of navigation clarity, progress states, and table workflows

## research-insight-1
Users needed reassurance that they could complete the process step by step instead of all at once.

## research-insight-2
People understood assets, contacts, and family as separate things until the product showed how they support will creation.

## research-insight-3
Progress visibility and plain-language guidance were more valuable than adding more educational content up front.

## results-body
The redesign made the product easier to begin and easier to trust. Users had a clearer sense of what the system was for, how far they had progressed, and why modules like Contacts, Family Tree, and Digital Vault mattered to the final will. The biggest win was not just a prettier dashboard. It was a product structure that turned a delayed, intimidating job into a sequence of smaller, more understandable tasks.

---

## reflections-body
Legacy Square reinforced an important product lesson for me: clarity matters more when the user is already emotionally resistant to the task. In these situations, good UX is not about adding excitement. It is about removing hesitation. The most useful design decisions in this project were the ones that made the product feel calmer, more connected, and more explicit about what happens next.
