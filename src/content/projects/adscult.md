## overview-summary
AdsCult is a self-serve performance advertising platform built for independent creators and small marketing agencies running paid campaigns across Meta, Google, and TikTok. The existing campaign creation flow had a 74% drop-off rate — most new users never launched their first campaign. I was brought in to redesign the onboarding and campaign setup experience from the ground up.

## overview-contribution
I owned the full UX process: stakeholder alignment, user research, information architecture restructure, interaction design, and prototype testing. I worked directly with the product lead and a front-end developer who joined in the final two weeks for the handoff sprint.

## problem-heading
The Problem

## problem-statement
New users were abandoning the platform before launching a single campaign — not because the product didn't work, but because the setup flow assumed they already knew how digital advertising worked.

## problem-body
The existing campaign creation wizard had 7 non-linear steps with no progress indicator, jargon-heavy labels ("CPM cap", "frequency ceiling", "objective taxonomy"), and no ability to save drafts. Users who got stuck had no contextual help — they had to leave the platform and search for answers externally. The result: a 74% drop-off before first campaign launch, and a flooded support inbox.

## problem-pain-1
The 7-step flow had no visible progress — users didn't know how far they were or how much was left, making it feel endless and arbitrary.

## problem-pain-2
Advertising jargon was used without explanation throughout — terms like "bid strategy" and "creative rotation" had no tooltips, glossary links, or examples.

## problem-pain-3
There was no draft-saving or resume functionality — if a user closed the tab mid-setup, they lost all progress and had to start over from scratch.

---

## requirements-intro
Three goals were aligned with the product team before any design work began, grounded in drop-off analytics and support ticket themes.

## requirement-1
Redesign the campaign setup as a linear, step-by-step guided flow with a persistent progress bar and the ability to save drafts at any point.

## requirement-2
Replace or contextually explain all advertising jargon with plain language — every technical term should have an inline explanation within 1 click.

## requirement-3
Reduce time-to-first-launched-campaign from the current average of 4 sessions to a single session for 80% of new users.

---

## research-intro
I ran a focused 10-day research sprint combining session replay analysis, moderated usability tests on the existing flow, and interviews with users who had churned within their first week.

## research-method-1
Session Replay Analysis (Hotjar, 200 sessions)

## research-method-2
Moderated Usability Tests (6 participants)

## research-method-3
Churn User Interviews (8 participants)

## research-method-4
Support Ticket Audit (120 tickets)

## research-insight-1
Session replays showed 68% of users who dropped off did so on step 4 ("Budget & Bidding") — the most jargon-heavy step with no explanatory copy or defaults.

## research-insight-2
In usability tests, 5 of 6 participants tried to click the platform logo to go back when they made a mistake — there was no back button or breadcrumb trail in the existing flow.

## research-insight-3
Support ticket audit revealed that 43% of all tickets were variations of "how do I launch my first campaign" — confirming that the problem was onboarding, not the ad product itself.

## wireframes-caption
Low-fidelity wireframes exploring a 4-step linear flow: Objective → Audience → Creative → Budget & Launch. Each step shows only the fields relevant to that decision. Inline contextual help cards replace jargon labels throughout.

## results-body
The redesigned onboarding launched to all new users in a phased rollout over 3 weeks. The single biggest impact came from the simplified step 4 — replacing the "Budget & Bidding" form with a three-option selector (Conservative, Balanced, Aggressive) with plain-language descriptions. Users who previously churned at this step now completed it in under 90 seconds on average.

## work-process
I used an iterative design process tightly coupled with the development team — shipping small improvements every week rather than a big redesign. This let us measure the impact of each change and course-correct quickly based on real user data.

## work-research
I interviewed 8 clients and 5 internal team members, reviewed 3 months of project management tool analytics to understand workflow bottlenecks, and benchmarked against 6 agency management platforms. The central finding: clients wanted visibility into project progress without having to ask for status updates, and the internal team needed fewer context switches between tools.

## work-ideation
I explored several approaches to the client dashboard — a Kanban-style project board, a timeline view, and a simplified status page with automated updates. Internal testing showed the status page approach reduced client check-in calls the most because it answered the "where are we?" question before it was asked.

## work-solutions
The redesigned platform introduced an automated client status page that pulled progress from internal project milestones, a unified inbox that consolidated feedback from email, Slack, and in-app comments into one thread per project, and a resource allocation view that helped the team balance workload across active projects.

## reflections-body
The most important lesson from this project: complexity in a product isn't always a feature — sometimes it's a symptom of unclear thinking about who the product is actually for. AdsCult had been designed by people who understood ad tech deeply, for users who didn't. Bridging that gap was mostly a language problem, not a UI problem.

I also learned that session replay analysis is massively underused as a UX research tool. It's not a replacement for usability testing, but it gave me quantitative confidence in where to focus the research — which made the 10-day sprint far more targeted than it would have been otherwise.

If I were to redo this project, I'd push for a longitudinal diary study with 5–6 new users over their first two weeks, to understand not just where they drop off but why they don't come back.
