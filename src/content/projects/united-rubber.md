## overview-summary
United Rubber is a mid-size rubber goods manufacturer supplying dealers and distributors across India. Their internal dealer portal — used daily by 200+ dealers to place bulk orders, track inventory, and manage invoices — was a legacy system built in 2014 with no significant UX updates. Task completion rates were low, support calls were high, and dealer satisfaction surveys showed recurring frustration with the ordering flow.

## overview-contribution
My role covered the full UX process: stakeholder interviews, dealer research, journey mapping, information architecture restructure, wireframing, and high-fidelity design. I collaborated closely with the product manager and two developers throughout.

## problem-heading
The Problem

## problem-statement
Dealers were spending 20+ minutes to place a standard bulk order — a task that should take under 5 minutes — because the portal's navigation, search, and order flow were designed around how the database was structured, not how dealers think.

## problem-body
The legacy portal had no meaningful information hierarchy. Dealers had to know exact product codes to place orders, the search returned unfiltered results with no sorting, and the checkout required 6 separate page loads with no progress indication. New dealers required a 2-hour onboarding session just to place their first order. This was costing the company in support overhead and dealer churn.

## problem-pain-1
Product search required exact SKU codes — dealers couldn't browse by category, material type, or application, which is how they naturally think about rubber products.

## problem-pain-2
The multi-page order flow had no persistent cart, so any session timeout lost all progress. Dealers reported rebuilding orders from scratch multiple times per week.

## problem-pain-3
Invoices and order history lived in a separate system accessed through a different login — dealers had to switch tools mid-task constantly.

---

## goals-intro
Three goals were aligned between UX, product, and the business before any design work began. These shaped every decision from IA through final screens.

## goal-1
Design a product catalogue that allows dealers to find products by application, material, or keyword — not just by SKU — reducing time-to-order-start to under 2 minutes.

## goal-2
Redesign the order flow as a persistent, single-session experience with a live cart, saved drafts, and clear progress indicators.

## goal-3
Consolidate invoices, order history, and account management into a unified portal so dealers never need to leave to complete a task.

## research-intro
Given the B2B context, I focused on contextual inquiry — watching dealers use the existing system in their actual work environment — combined with stakeholder interviews with the sales and support teams who heard dealer feedback daily.

## research-method-1
Contextual Inquiry (6 dealer visits)

## research-method-2
Stakeholder Interviews (Sales & Support)

## research-method-3
Hotjar Session Recordings Analysis

## research-method-4
Card Sorting (IA restructure)

## research-insight-1
During contextual inquiry, I observed dealers keeping a separate Excel sheet of their frequently ordered products because the portal search was too slow. This became the design brief for a "Quick Reorder" feature.

## research-insight-2
Support call logs showed that 43% of dealer calls were about order status — information that existed in the system but was buried 4 clicks deep with no notifications.

## research-insight-3
Card sorting with 14 dealers revealed that they categorised rubber products primarily by application (automotive, industrial, household) — the opposite of the database's category structure (material > grade > size).

## results-body
The redesigned portal launched in a phased rollout — first to 40 pilot dealers, then to the full network over 4 weeks. The most impactful single change was the Quick Reorder feature on the dashboard: dealers with an existing order history were able to reorder in under 3 minutes from day one. The support team reported an immediate drop in order-related calls within the first two weeks.

## work-process
I adopted a lean UX process — short research sprints followed by rapid prototyping and testing cycles. Given the B2B context, I worked closely with the sales team to understand buyer workflows and pain points that were not visible in analytics alone.

## work-research
I interviewed 10 procurement managers across different industries, shadowed 3 sales calls to observe how buyers discussed product specifications, and analysed 6 months of support tickets to identify recurring friction points. The key finding: buyers needed to compare product specs side-by-side before requesting a quote.

## work-ideation
I mapped out the buyer journey from product discovery to quote request and identified 4 key decision moments. I sketched multiple approaches for the product comparison feature — modal overlay, side-by-side table, and a pinned comparison bar. User testing with 6 procurement managers showed the pinned bar approach felt most natural.

## work-solutions
The redesigned catalogue introduced filterable product grids with technical spec sheets, a sticky comparison bar that let buyers pin up to 3 products, and a streamlined quote request form that pre-filled product details. The enquiry-to-quote conversion improved significantly within the first quarter.

## reflections-body
This project reinforced that B2B UX requires extra investment in contextual research. Reading session recordings or survey responses alone would never have surfaced the Excel "workaround" that dealers were using — that came from sitting with them at their desks.

The IA restructure was the highest-leverage design decision. Changing the product catalogue from SKU-first to application-first wasn't technically complex, but it required buy-in from the product and engineering teams who had built around the database structure. The card sorting data was essential for that conversation.

One thing I'd do differently: involve the development team earlier in the IA restructure to understand backend constraints. We hit two instances where a clean UX pattern required non-trivial backend changes, which caused delays in the final sprint.
