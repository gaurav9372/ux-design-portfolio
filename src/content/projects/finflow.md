## overview-summary
FinFlow is a personal finance app targeting young professionals who want to build better money habits. Despite strong initial downloads, the app had a day-30 retention rate of just 12% — users set it up, categorised a few transactions, and never returned. I led a full UX overhaul focused on the onboarding flow, the core budgeting loop, and the daily "check-in" interaction that was supposed to drive habit formation but wasn't working.

## overview-contribution
I led user research, interaction design, and the design system for this project. I worked alongside a product manager who handled stakeholder alignment and a UI designer who refined the visual polish in the final two weeks. All core UX decisions — research synthesis, flows, wireframes, and prototype — were mine.

## problem-heading
The Problem

## problem-statement
Users were setting up FinFlow, categorising their first few transactions, and then never opening the app again — not because budgeting was hard, but because the app gave them no compelling reason to return the next day.

## problem-body
The core habit loop was broken. The app tracked spending passively but never gave users a meaningful moment of engagement — no summary, no insight, no small win to celebrate. The onboarding asked users to set budgets immediately, before they had any context about their spending patterns. And the daily check-in was just a push notification that opened to a transaction list — not a designed experience. Most users turned off notifications within 72 hours.

## problem-pain-1
The onboarding flow asked users to set monthly budgets on day one — before they had seen any of their spending data. Most users guessed or skipped, leading to budgets that were immediately irrelevant.

## problem-pain-2
The app had no "moment of delight" — no weekly summary, no milestone celebration, no insight that surprised the user. It felt like a spreadsheet, not a habit app.

## problem-pain-3
The transaction categorisation was fully manual and required 3 taps per transaction. Users with 30–50 monthly transactions gave up within the first week.

---

## requirements-intro
Three design goals were set after synthesising research — each targeting a specific point in the broken habit loop.

## requirement-1
Redesign onboarding to defer budget-setting until after the user has connected their account and seen their first spending summary — letting data inform their targets.

## requirement-2
Design a daily check-in experience that gives users one meaningful insight in under 30 seconds — replacing the push notification → transaction list flow.

## requirement-3
Introduce smart auto-categorisation with one-tap correction — reducing the manual categorisation load from 3 taps to 1 for at least 70% of transactions.

## research-intro
I used a combination of diary studies and behavioural analytics to understand not just where users dropped off, but what their relationship with money management looked like before they downloaded FinFlow.

## research-method-1
Diary Study (10 participants, 2 weeks)

## research-method-2
User Interviews (12 participants)

## research-method-3
Behavioural Analytics Review (Mixpanel)

## research-method-4
Competitive Analysis (Walnut, YNAB, Jupiter)

## research-insight-1
Diary study entries showed that 8 of 10 participants had a habitual moment of "checking their phone" in the morning before work — but this habit was tied to social apps, not finance. The opportunity was to attach FinFlow to an existing habit slot.

## research-insight-2
Analytics showed that users who categorised more than 10 transactions in their first session had 4x higher day-7 retention — but the manual categorisation friction meant most users gave up after 3–4 transactions.

## research-insight-3
In interviews, every participant described a moment in the past when they were surprised by their bank statement — "I had no idea I was spending that much on food." This surprise was the emotional trigger we needed to design for — proactively, not reactively.

Wireframes exploring a redesigned onboarding: account connection first, then a "Your first spending snapshot" screen before any budget-setting. The daily check-in was prototyped as a full-screen card with a single insight, one action, and a dismiss — designed to be completable in under 30 seconds.

## results-body
The redesigned app launched in a phased rollout — first to a cohort of 500 beta users, then to all users over 4 weeks. The biggest single win was the "morning snapshot" daily check-in — a single card summarising yesterday's spending vs the user's target, with one actionable insight. It replaced the push notification → transaction list experience entirely. Day-7 retention for the beta cohort was 71%, compared to 34% for the control group.

## work-process
I followed a structured discovery-to-delivery process with extra emphasis on data visualisation best practices. Financial dashboards demand precision, so every chart type and data layout went through multiple rounds of review with actual finance teams before finalising.

## work-research
I interviewed 12 finance professionals across startups and mid-sized companies, observed 5 participants navigating their current expense tracking tools, and audited 7 competing financial management platforms. The standout finding: most users did not need more data — they needed fewer, more actionable insights surfaced at the right time.

## work-ideation
I explored 3 dashboard concepts — a traditional table-heavy layout, a card-based summary view, and a narrative-driven approach that told a "financial story" with progressive disclosure. Testing with 8 users showed the card-based view with drill-down capability struck the right balance between overview and detail.

## work-solutions
The final dashboard used a card-based layout with 4 key financial health indicators at the top, smart alerts for unusual spending patterns, and a transaction timeline with natural language search. I designed a budget vs. actual view that used colour-coded progress bars instead of complex charts, making it instantly readable.

## reflections-body
The most valuable research method in this project was the diary study. Seeing how users related to money day-to-day — separate from the app entirely — gave me insight into emotional patterns that no usability test would have surfaced. Understanding that users felt guilt, not curiosity, when looking at their spending changed the entire tone of the product design.

The decision to defer budget-setting until after the first spending snapshot was initially met with pushback from the product team, who worried it would reduce activation rates. The data proved the opposite — completion of onboarding went up because users were seeing something relevant to them before being asked to commit.

If I did this again, I'd involve the engineering team in the research synthesis. The auto-categorisation feature was technically constrained in ways that affected the design — if I'd known those constraints earlier, I'd have designed a different solution from the start rather than having to iterate late in the sprint.
