## overview-summary
Flavor Street is a hyperlocal food discovery app focused entirely on street food and small, unlisted eateries — a category that mainstream food apps like Zomato and Swiggy largely ignore because these vendors don't have delivery infrastructure. The project involved designing the full discovery, search, vendor profile, and save/share experience from zero, with equal attention to both the customer-facing app and the vendor onboarding flow.

## overview-contribution
I led all UX work across both the customer and vendor sides of the product. This included competitive research, user and vendor interviews, user flow design, information architecture, wireframing, and high-fidelity prototyping. I collaborated with a visual designer who handled the final brand language and a product manager who managed vendor acquisition partnerships.

## problem-heading
The Problem

## problem-statement
The best street food in any city is invisible online — passed between friends by word of mouth, found by accident, and lost when a stall moves. There was no dedicated way to discover, save, or share these vendors, and the vendors themselves had no way to build an audience beyond foot traffic.

## problem-body
Mainstream food apps exclude street vendors because they don't offer delivery and can't afford commission structures. Google Maps has listing gaps and inaccurate information for unlisted stalls. The result: a significant portion of a city's food culture is effectively undiscoverable if you're not already a local. Flavor Street's founder saw this gap and wanted to build a product that served both sides — food explorers who wanted to find authentic, off-menu experiences, and vendors who wanted digital visibility without the overhead of a full restaurant listing.

## problem-pain-1
Discovery on existing platforms requires knowing what you're looking for — there was no way to browse by experience type (e.g., "late-night momos in Lajpat Nagar") or discover vendors based on what was open right now, nearby.

## problem-pain-2
Vendor information on Google Maps was frequently outdated — wrong timings, wrong location (stalls that had moved), and no indication of seasonal menus or daily specials.

## problem-pain-3
There was no trusted social layer for this type of food — no way to see where friends had eaten or share a find without screenshotting a Google Maps pin and sending it on WhatsApp.

---

## goals-intro
Three goals were aligned with the founding team after research — each addressing a distinct failure mode of existing discovery tools.

## goal-1
Design a discovery experience built around real-world browsing behaviour — proximity, what's open now, and experience type — not keyword search alone.

## goal-2
Build a vendor profile structure that surface timings, location accuracy, and daily specials as first-class information — the three things users cared most about in research.

## goal-3
Design a save-and-share layer that makes passing on a food find as easy as sharing an Instagram story — reducing the friction between discovery and recommendation.

## research-intro
I ran research on both sides of the marketplace — understanding food explorers and street vendors separately before designing the connection between them.

## research-method-1
User Interviews — Food Explorers (14 participants)

## research-method-2
Vendor Interviews (10 vendors, 3 cities)

## research-method-3
Observational Research (Street Food Markets, 3 visits)

## research-method-4
Competitor Audit (Zomato, Google Maps, Dineout, Instagram)

## research-insight-1
In interviews, 11 of 14 food explorers described discovering their favourite street food spots through a friend's recommendation or by walking past them — not through any app. The design challenge was to replicate the "tip from a friend" dynamic digitally.

## research-insight-2
Vendor interviews revealed that the biggest barrier to going digital was time and complexity — vendors had tried setting up Google Business profiles but found the process too complicated and maintenance too demanding. A simplified, WhatsApp-native vendor onboarding was a direct outcome of this finding.

## research-insight-3
Observational research at street food markets showed that groups of people frequently split up to explore different stalls and then reconvened to share what they'd found. This "explore and report back" behaviour directly shaped the group planning and sharing features.

## wireframes-caption
Low-fidelity wireframes exploring the map-first vs. list-first discovery debate — we tested both with 8 users. Map-first won significantly for exploratory sessions, list-first won for intent-based search. The final design uses a hybrid: list view with a persistent map toggle, defaulting to map when location permission is active.

## screens-caption
Final screens delivered in Figma for iOS and Android. Included vendor profile templates, the discovery map view, the save/share flow, and the simplified vendor onboarding flow. Full design system with 60+ components.

## results-body
The beta launched in two cities with 800 users and 120 vendor listings. The discovery-to-save rate (82%) significantly exceeded the product team's 50% target. The map-first discovery flow generated the longest session times — users spent an average of 6.4 minutes per session exploring the map, compared to 3.2 minutes on the list view. The vendor onboarding completion rate was 74%, compared to a 23% completion rate the team had seen with an earlier self-serve Google Form approach.

## reflections-body
This project taught me the value of designing for both sides of a marketplace simultaneously — something I hadn't done before. The vendor onboarding flow was initially treated as an afterthought, but research made clear that the product's supply quality would determine its demand-side experience. You can't design a great discovery app if the listings are sparse or inaccurate.

The map vs. list decision was also a meaningful reminder to test assumptions rather than go with convention. The team initially assumed list-first because most food apps use it. Data said map-first for this use case, and the final product is meaningfully better for it.

One thing I'd do differently: I'd run a longitudinal retention study rather than relying on beta satisfaction scores. Satisfaction in the first week doesn't predict whether users come back in week 4 — and for a discovery app, return visits are the real metric.
