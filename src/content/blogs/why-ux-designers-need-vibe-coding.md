## body
There is a new term floating around the design and tech world right now. Vibe coding. If you have not heard it yet, here is the short version. It is the idea of building functional things by describing what you want in plain language to an AI tool, and the AI writes the code for you. You guide it, you tweak it, you steer the direction. But you are not writing code line by line. You are coding by vibes.

And I think every UX designer needs to learn it. Not tomorrow. Now.

Before you roll your eyes and say "I did not become a designer to write code," hear me out. What I am suggesting has nothing to do with becoming a frontend developer. It is something far more practical than that.

1. The gap between design and development is where good ideas go to die

You have been there. You design something beautiful and thoughtful in Figma. You hand it off. Two weeks later you see the built version and it looks nothing like what you designed. The spacing is off. The interaction feels wrong. That microanimation you carefully planned? Gone.

This happens because there is a translation layer between design and code, and that layer loses information every single time. Vibe coding lets you skip that layer. Not completely, not for production code, but for prototypes that actually work. Real prototypes with real interactions, not just clickable Figma frames.

When you can build a working prototype yourself, you stop depending on developers to validate your ideas. You can test real interactions with real users before a single sprint is planned.

2. Prototypes in Figma are not real and everyone knows it

Let me be honest about something. Figma prototypes are good for showing screen flow. Click here, go there, see this. But they cannot show how a drag interaction feels. They cannot show what happens when data loads slowly. They cannot simulate a form that validates in real time. They cannot show a list that scrolls with momentum and snaps to items.

These are the details that make or break a user experience. And right now, the only way to test them is to wait for a developer to build them. By then, the design is locked and nobody wants to change anything.

With vibe coding, you can describe what you want to an AI coding tool and get a working HTML page in minutes. It will not be production quality. It does not need to be. It just needs to be real enough to test with users and show stakeholders how something actually feels, not just how it looks.

3. Stakeholders trust working things more than mockups

This is the uncomfortable truth nobody talks about. When you show a Figma prototype in a meeting, people nod politely. When you show something that actually works in a browser, people lean forward. They click around. They get excited. They ask "when can we ship this?"

I have seen this happen multiple times now. The same design concept gets a lukewarm response as a Figma prototype and an enthusiastic response as a rough but functional web page. People trust what they can touch more than what they can only look at.

Vibe coding gives you that power. You do not need to know React or write CSS from scratch. You describe what you want, the AI builds it, and you adjust until it feels right. The result is something real that runs in a browser, and that changes how people respond to your work.

4. You start understanding developer constraints for real

Every designer says they understand developer constraints. Most of us are lying. We understand them in theory. "Oh yeah, that animation might be hard to build." But we do not really feel it until we try to build something ourselves.

When you vibe code, you hit real walls. You realize that the layout you designed does not work when the content is dynamic. You discover that the interaction you imagined is actually three separate states that need to be handled differently. You learn that "just make it responsive" involves fifty decisions you never thought about.

It sounds frustrating but it is actually educational. And it makes you a dramatically better designer because you start designing things that are buildable from the start instead of throwing impossible ideas over the wall and hoping for the best.

5. AI tools have made the barrier almost zero

Two years ago, if I told a designer to "learn coding," that meant spending months on HTML, CSS, and JavaScript tutorials. That is a huge ask when you already have a full time job doing design work.

Today the barrier is almost gone. You open Claude or Cursor or Bolt or Replit and you say "build me a signup form with email validation, a password strength meter, and a success animation." And it builds it. In seconds. You look at it, say "make the button bigger and add more spacing between the fields," and it updates.

You are not learning syntax. You are not memorizing functions. You are describing what you want and steering the output. That is a fundamentally different skill than traditional coding, and it is one that designers are already good at because we think in terms of outcomes and experiences.

6. Your portfolio will look completely different

Right now, most UX portfolios look the same. Figma screens, user flows, some persona documents, a few before and after comparisons. They are fine. But they do not stand out.

Imagine a portfolio where every case study includes a working prototype. Not screenshots of a prototype. An actual link someone can click and interact with. That is a completely different level of credibility. It shows that you do not just think about experiences, you can build them.

I started adding working demos to my case studies a few months ago and the response has been noticeably different. Hiring managers spend more time on my portfolio. They ask more questions. They remember specific interactions I built rather than just the visual design.

7. The designers who do not learn this will fall behind

I do not say this to scare you. I say it because the industry is moving in this direction whether we like it or not. Companies are already looking for designers who can prototype in code. Job descriptions are already listing "familiarity with HTML/CSS" or "ability to create functional prototypes" as preferred qualifications.

Vibe coding makes meeting those requirements realistic for any designer. You do not need a computer science degree. You do not need to understand algorithms. You need to be able to describe what you want clearly and iterate on the output. That is literally what we do all day as designers.

The designers who embrace this will ship faster, communicate better with developers, and produce work that feels more real at every stage of the process. The ones who resist it will increasingly find themselves waiting on other people to bring their ideas to life.

How to actually start

Do not overthink this. Here is what I did.

I picked a simple screen from one of my projects. A login page. Nothing fancy. I opened Claude and said "build me a login page with an email field, password field, and a submit button. Dark background, clean and minimal." It gave me working HTML and CSS in about 10 seconds.

Then I started tweaking. "Add a show password toggle." "Make the button full width." "Add a subtle box shadow to the card." "Show an error state when the email is invalid." Each time, I described what I wanted and it updated the code.

Within an hour, I had a fully functional login page with validation, error states, loading animation, and responsive layout. It was not perfect. But it was real. And building it taught me more about how interfaces actually work than any tutorial ever did.

Start there. Pick something simple. Describe it to an AI. See what comes out. Then iterate. That is all vibe coding is. And once you get comfortable with it, you will wonder how you ever designed without it.

Wrapping up

Vibe coding is here to stay. It is the beginning of a fundamental shift in how design and development work together. The wall between "people who design" and "people who build" is getting thinner every day, and AI is the reason.

You do not need to become a developer. You do not need to write code from scratch. You do not need to understand what a JavaScript promise is or how CSS grid works under the hood. You just need to be willing to describe what you want and work with what you get.

That is a skill every designer already has. The only question is whether you are going to use it. And from where I am standing, the answer should be obvious.
