## body
When I started designing, I thought I was doing everything right. I was watching YouTube tutorials, recreating Dribbble shots, and collecting Figma plugins like they were Pokemon cards. Turns out, I was making a bunch of mistakes that nobody told me about until much later.

So here are 12 things I wish someone had told me on day one. Not from some design textbook. Just stuff I learned by messing up, getting feedback, and slowly getting better.

1. Designing for yourself instead of the user

This is the big one. You open Figma, pick your favorite colors, choose a trendy font, and start building something that looks good to you. But here is the thing. You are not the user. Your mom is not the user. Your designer friend on Twitter is not the user.

The fix is simple but uncomfortable. Talk to real people who will actually use the product. Watch them struggle. Ask them what confuses them. It changes everything.

2. Skipping research because it feels slow

I get it. Research sounds boring when you just want to push pixels. But designing without research is like cooking without tasting the food. You might get lucky, but most of the time you will end up with something nobody wants.

You do not need a fancy research setup. Even five quick interviews or reading through customer support tickets will give you more insight than guessing.

3. Making everything look pretty before it works

This one hurt me personally. I used to spend hours picking the perfect gradient for a button before I even figured out what the button was supposed to do. Pretty screens that confuse people are worse than ugly screens that make sense.

Start ugly. Sketch on paper. Use wireframes. Get the flow right first. Then make it beautiful.

4. Ignoring typography and spacing

This is the stuff that separates good design from great design, and most juniors completely overlook it. You will spend an hour choosing between two shades of blue but give your text random font sizes and spacing.

Learn the basics. Pick two fonts max. Use a consistent type scale. Give your text room to breathe. Whitespace is not wasted space. It is what makes things readable.

5. Not asking for feedback early enough

I used to work on a design for weeks, polishing every pixel, and then show it to my team expecting applause. Instead I got "this does not solve the problem we talked about." That stung.

Show your work early, even when it is rough. The earlier you get feedback, the less time you waste going in the wrong direction. Nobody expects your first draft to be perfect.

6. Trying to reinvent standard patterns

You know that hamburger menu icon? There is a reason everyone uses it. Users already know what it means. When you try to replace it with some creative icon you came up with, you are just making people think harder.

Use standard patterns for standard things. Save your creativity for the parts of the product where it actually matters. Nobody will thank you for a clever new way to close a popup.

7. Designing only the happy path

Your design looks great when everything goes right. But what happens when the user types the wrong password? What does the empty state look like? What if their name is 47 characters long?

Real users do unexpected things. Your designs need to handle errors, empty states, loading states, and edge cases. These are the moments where users actually need good design the most.

8. Collecting tools instead of building skills

New Figma plugin drops? Installed. New prototyping tool? Downloaded. New icon pack? Saved to bookmarks. Sound familiar?

Tools are nice but they do not make you a better designer. Understanding layout, hierarchy, and user behavior does. I have seen amazing designers who only use Figma with zero plugins. Focus on the thinking, not the toolbox.

9. Comparing your day one to someone else's year five

You see a senior designer post a beautiful case study and think "I will never be that good." But you are looking at their highlight reel. You did not see the 50 bad versions they made before that one.

Everyone starts somewhere. The designers you admire were once exactly where you are. Just keep making things and learning from each project.

10. Not learning to present your work

You could have the best design in the room, but if you cannot explain why you made the choices you did, people will not trust it. Presenting is a skill, and it matters just as much as the design itself.

Practice talking through your decisions. "I chose this layout because users told us they scan from top to bottom" is way more convincing than "I thought it looked nice."

11. Treating every project like a portfolio piece

When you are starting out, it is tempting to over design everything so it looks good in your portfolio. But in a real job, sometimes the best design is the simplest one. The one that ships fast and solves the problem without drama.

Not everything needs to be a showstopper. Sometimes a clean, simple solution is exactly what the project needs. Your portfolio will thank you for showing restraint and good judgment.

12. Giving up too early when things get hard

Design is hard. There are days when nothing works, when stakeholders reject your ideas, when you feel like you are not improving. That is normal. Every designer goes through it.

The ones who get good are the ones who keep showing up. Keep designing. Keep asking questions. Keep learning from your mistakes. That is literally all there is to it.

Wrapping up

So if you are a junior designer reading this, do not stress too much. You are going to make mistakes. That is part of the process. The goal is not to be perfect. The goal is to get a little better every week.

The difference between designers who grow fast and those who stay stuck is not talent. It is awareness. Once you know what the common mistakes look like, you start catching yourself before you fall into them. You start asking better questions. You start thinking about the user before you think about the pixels.

None of these 12 things are hard to fix on their own. The hard part is admitting you are doing them in the first place. But once you do that, everything else gets easier. And if even one of these points saves you from a mistake I made, then this whole article was worth writing.
