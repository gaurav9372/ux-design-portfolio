## body
Let me be honest. The relationship between designers and developers is not always smooth. People write about collaboration like it is some magical thing where everyone high fives and ships perfect products. That is not how it goes in real life.

In real life, you hand off a design and the developer says "this is not possible in the timeline we have." Or you open the staging link and nothing looks like what you designed. Or worse, nobody tells you they changed something until it is already live.

I have been on both sides of this. And most of the friction comes from a few very specific places.

1. You design things without knowing what is hard to build

This is the number one source of tension. You spend a week on a beautiful interaction, hand it off, and the developer tells you it would take three sprints just to build that one animation. Now you are frustrated because you feel like your work was wasted. And the developer is frustrated because they feel like you do not understand their constraints.

The fix is boring but it works. Talk to your developer before you start designing. Not after. Ask them what is easy to build, what is tricky, and what is a nightmare. You do not need to understand code. You just need to understand effort.

2. Your Figma file is a mess

Developers do not read your mind. If your layers are named "Frame 847" and your components are scattered across 12 pages with no order, you are making their job harder. And when their job is harder, they start guessing. And when they guess, things look wrong.

Name your layers. Use auto layout. Keep your components consistent. Add notes where things are unclear. Think of your Figma file as a document that someone else needs to read, not just something you look at.

3. You treat the handoff as the finish line

Most designers think the job is done when you send the Figma link. It is not. The handoff is where the real work begins. Developers will have questions. Things will need to change. Some details will get lost in translation.

Stay involved after handoff. Check the build regularly. Give feedback early when something looks off, not two days before launch. The best design relationships I have had were ones where I sat with the developer and we figured things out together on the spot.

4. You do not speak their language (and you do not try to)

You do not need to learn JavaScript. But understanding basic concepts like how components work in code, what responsive means from a developer's perspective, and why certain layouts are harder than others will save you a lot of back and forth.

When you say "just make it responsive" and the developer asks "what should happen at 768 pixels?" and you shrug, that is a problem. You should know the answer. Or at least have thought about it.

5. You take feedback on implementation personally

When a developer says "we cannot do this exactly as designed," it is not an attack on your work. It is a reality of building software. Timelines, technical debt, and browser quirks all push back on even the cleanest designs.

The designers who are great to work with are the ones who hear that feedback and say "okay, what can we do that is close?" instead of digging in and insisting on pixel perfection at the cost of shipping on time.

6. You do not document edge cases

Your design looks perfect with a name that is 10 characters long. But what happens when someone's name is 45 characters? What does the empty state look like? What if the API is slow and data takes 5 seconds to load?

Developers have to handle all of these scenarios whether you design for them or not. If you do not provide direction, they will make their own decisions. And you might not love those decisions.

7. You never ask for their input on the design

Here is something that surprised me early in my career. Developers often have great design ideas. They see the product from a completely different angle. They know what users complain about in support tickets. They know which features are used the most.

When you include developers in design reviews or brainstorming sessions, two things happen. They feel ownership over the product, which means they care more about getting the details right. And you get insights you would have missed on your own.

The truth nobody tells you

The friction between designers and developers is rarely about personality. It is almost always about communication. Most of the time, both sides want the same thing, a good product that ships on time. The disconnect happens because nobody set up a good process for talking to each other.

Wrapping up

The shortest version of this article is one line. The best design happens when designers and developers actually talk to each other. Not through Jira tickets. Not through Figma comments. Actually talk.

Go sit with your developer this week. Not to hand something off. Just to talk. Ask them what annoys them about the current process. Ask them what would make their life easier. You will be surprised how much better things get when you just ask.

Most of the problems I described in this article disappear the moment both sides feel like they are on the same team. Because they are. You both want the same thing. You just need a better way to get there together.
