## body
I started using Claude about six months ago. Not because I wanted it to design things for me. I was just stuck on a project and needed someone to think out loud with. My team was busy, my manager was in back to back meetings, and I had a research synthesis that was going nowhere.

So I opened Claude and just started talking through the problem. And something clicked. Not because Claude gave me the answer. But because explaining my problem to it forced me to think more clearly about what I was actually trying to solve.

That was the moment I stopped thinking of AI as a tool and started thinking of it as a thinking partner. And honestly, it has changed how I work more than any Figma plugin ever has.

1. Talking through messy research findings

After user interviews, my notes are always a mess. Quotes everywhere, half formed observations, things that feel important but I cannot explain why yet. Before Claude, I would stare at my Miro board for hours trying to force patterns out of the chaos.

Now I dump my raw notes into Claude and say something like "I just did 8 interviews about onboarding. Here are my notes. What patterns do you see?" And it gives me a first pass. Sometimes it catches things I missed. Sometimes it groups things in ways I would not have thought of.

But here is the important part. I never take its synthesis as the final answer. I use it as a starting point for my own thinking. It is like having a research assistant who reads fast and organizes well but does not have the context I have from actually being in the room with the users.

2. Stress testing my design decisions

This is probably my favorite use case. I finish a design and instead of just moving on, I describe it to Claude and ask "what could go wrong with this approach?" or "what am I not thinking about?"

It will come back with edge cases I forgot, accessibility concerns I overlooked, or scenarios where my flow breaks down. Last week it pointed out that my onboarding flow assumed users would set up their profile immediately, but what about users who just want to explore first? I had not thought about that. It was a real gap.

It is like having a design reviewer who is always available and never tired. Not as good as a senior designer reviewing your work, but way better than no review at all.

3. Writing first drafts of UX copy that actually sound human

I hate writing microcopy. Error messages, empty states, confirmation dialogs. I know they matter. I just do not enjoy writing them. So I tell Claude the context and ask for options.

"The user just tried to delete their account but they have an active subscription. Write 5 versions of an error message. Keep it friendly, not corporate. Max 2 sentences."

Most of the options are decent. One or two are usually good enough to use as a base. I always rewrite them in my own voice, but starting from something is so much easier than starting from nothing.

The trick is being really specific about context and tone. If you just say "write an error message," you will get something generic and robotic. The more context you give, the better the output.

4. Exploring problem spaces before jumping into solutions

This is where Claude changed my process the most. I used to jump into Figma way too early. Someone would describe a problem and I would immediately start sketching screens. Now I spend 20 minutes talking through the problem with Claude first.

I ask things like "what are the different ways this problem could be solved?" or "what would a user who is frustrated with this flow actually want?" or "what assumptions am I making here that might be wrong?"

It does not always give brilliant answers. But the act of asking these questions before I start designing means I show up to Figma with a much clearer head. I waste less time on directions that do not lead anywhere.

5. Generating personas from real interview data

I feed Claude my interview transcripts and ask it to identify distinct user types. It pulls out behavioral patterns, common goals, and recurring frustrations. Then I use that as the skeleton for my persona documents.

The key word is skeleton. I always add the human details myself. The little observations that did not fit neatly into a pattern. The way one user laughed nervously when talking about their budgeting habits. The fact that another user mentioned they only use the app on their commute. Those details make personas feel real, and AI cannot capture them because it was not in the room.

6. Debugging my information architecture

When I am working on navigation or page structure, I describe the current IA to Claude and ask it to poke holes in it. "If a user is looking for their billing settings, would they find it under Account or Settings? What about users who think of it as Payment?"

It helps me see my labels and categories from an outside perspective. Sometimes I am too close to the product to realize that the words I am using make sense to the team but not to a new user. Claude does not have that internal bias, so it catches naming issues faster than I do.

7. Preparing for stakeholder presentations

Before a big presentation, I walk Claude through my design rationale and ask it to play devil's advocate. "If you were a product manager who cares about shipping fast, what would you push back on?" or "If you were an engineer, what would concern you about this design?"

This is not about getting perfect rebuttals. It is about anticipating questions so I am not caught off guard. The worst feeling in a design review is someone asking a question you have not thought about and having nothing to say. Claude helps me show up prepared.

What I do not use it for

I do not use Claude to generate layouts or visual design. I tried it a few times and the suggestions were too generic to be useful. Design is about context and nuance and trade offs that you can only understand when you are deep inside a project. AI is not there yet.

I also do not use it for final decisions. Claude can help me think through options, but the decision is always mine. I know my users. I know my team. I know the business constraints. Claude does not.

And I never use it during user interviews or testing. Those moments need to be fully human. The whole point of research is to connect with real people, and you cannot do that if you are distracted by a chatbot.

How to start if you are curious

Do not try to integrate AI into your entire workflow at once. Pick one thing. For me it was research synthesis. For you it might be copy writing or decision stress testing or presentation prep.

Use it for a week on that one thing. See if it actually saves you time or helps you think better. If it does, keep it. If it does not, try something else. There is no magic formula here. It is just another tool in your process, and like any tool, it works better in some situations than others.

Wrapping up

The biggest shift for me was not about what Claude can do. It was about how I work with it. When I treat it like a vending machine where I put in a prompt and expect a perfect output, it is disappointing. When I treat it like a thinking partner where I talk through problems and use its responses to sharpen my own thinking, it is genuinely useful.

I still do all the actual design work myself. I still make all the decisions. I still sit with users and listen to their problems. None of that has changed. What has changed is that I spend less time stuck. Less time staring at a blank screen wondering where to start. Less time second guessing myself in isolation.

And honestly, that alone makes it worth it. Not because AI is magic. But because sometimes you just need someone to think with. Even if that someone is not a person.
